Definition (Canonical)

BCC (Bidirectional Conceptual Coupling) is the theoretical framework explaining how Sino-judicial activism shapes DIPR (Data Intellectual Property Rights). It consists of two complementary discourses:

  • Judicial Continuation (Inside-Out): Deployed by local courts (e.g., Zhejiang High People's Court, HCZJ) to extend existing IP norms to data disputes (treating data "as if" it were property).
  • Judicial Linkage (Outside-In): Deployed by the Supreme People's Court (SPC) to align adjudication with state policy and administrative governance (treating data "as" policy).

The interaction between these two discourses legitimizes Data IP protection while constraining platform monopolization.

abstract

This paper uses Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA) to show how Sino-judicial activism shapes Data Intellectual Property Rights (DIPR) in China. We identify two complementary judicial discourses.

Local courts (exemplified by the Zhejiang High People's Court, HCZJ) use a judicial continuation discourse that extends intellectual property norms to data disputes. The Supreme People's Court (SPC) deploys a judicial linkage discourse that aligns adjudication with state policy and administrative governance.

Their interaction forms a Bidirectional Conceptual Coupling (BCC): an inside-out projection of local reasoning and an outside-in translation of policy into doctrine. The coupling both legitimizes and constrains courts and policy-makers, balancing pressure for unified market standards with safeguards against platform monopolization. Through cases such as HCZJ's Taobao v. Meijing and the SPC's Anti-Unfair Competition Interpretation, the study presents DIPR as a testbed for doctrinal innovation and institutional coordination in China's evolving digital governance.

abstract

Traditional fair use can no longer respond to the challenges posed by generative AI. Drawing on a comparative analysis of China's Ultraman and the U.S. cases Bartz v. Anthropic and Kadrey v. Meta, this article proposes "Develop-Fair Use" (DFU). DFU treats AI fair use (AIFU) not as a fixed exception but as a dynamic tool of judicial balancing that shifts analysis from closed scenarios to an evaluative rule for open-ended contexts.

The judicial focus moves from formal classification of facts to a substantive balancing of competition in relevant markets. Although China and the U.S. follow different paths, both reveal this logic: Ultraman, by articulating a "four-context analysis," creates institutional space for AI industry development; the debate over the fourth factor, market impact, in the two U.S. cases, especially Kadrey's "market dilution" claim, expands review from substitution in copyright markets to wider industrial competition.

The core of DFU is to recognize and balance the tension in relevant markets between an emerging AI industry that invokes fair use to build its markets and a publishing industry that develops markets, including one for "training licenses," to resist fair use. The boundary of fair use is therefore not a product of pure legal deduction but a case-specific factual judgment grounded in evolving market realities. This approach aims both to trim excess copyright scope and to remedy shortfalls in market competition.

key contributions

  • DFU proposal: Introduces Develop-Fair Use (DFU) as a framework that treats AI fair use (AIFU) as a dynamic tool of judicial balancing, not a fixed exception.
  • Shift in analytic center: Re-frames adjudication from classifying facts in closed scenarios to evaluating open-ended contexts.
  • Market-competition orientation: Moves the judicial focus toward substantive balancing of competition in relevant markets, rather than only substitution within copyright markets.
  • Comparative doctrinal leverage: Uses China's Ultraman ("four-context analysis") and U.S. case debates (especially Kadrey's "market dilution") to surface a shared underlying logic despite different legal paths.
  • Industry tension mapping: Identifies the key tension between an emerging AI industry invoking fair use to build markets and a publishing industry developing markets (including "training licenses") to resist fair use.
  • Normative aim: Seeks to trim excess copyright scope while also remedying shortfalls in market competition.

definitions (glossary)

  • DFU (Develop-Fair Use): A theory that treats AIFU as a dynamic, case-specific tool of judicial balancing, oriented to evolving market realities and competition in relevant markets.
  • AIFU (AI fair use): Fair use questions arising in AI contexts, especially model training and related downstream uses.
  • Relevant markets: The set of markets whose competitive dynamics should be balanced in the fair use analysis, potentially beyond traditional copyright licensing markets.
  • Four-context analysis: The analytic approach articulated in China's Ultraman case that creates institutional space for AI industry development.
  • Market dilution: A claim (highlighted in Kadrey v. Meta discussions) that expands review from substitution in copyright markets to broader industrial competition impacts.
  • Training licenses: A market mechanism developed by the publishing industry to resist fair use by offering licenses for AI training-related uses.
